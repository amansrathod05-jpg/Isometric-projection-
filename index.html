Introduction to Isometric Projection

Isometric projection is a method of visually representing three-dimensional objects in two dimensions. It belongs to the family of axonometric projections, where the object is rotated along one or more axes relative to the plane of projection.

In isometric projection, the three coordinate axes appear equally foreshortened, and the angle between any two axes is exactly 120 degrees. This creates a visually balanced and measurable representation.
  • The word "isometric" comes from Greek: iso (equal) + metron (measure)
  • All three axes are equally inclined to the plane of projection at ~35.27 degrees
  • It is one of the three types of axonometric projections: isometric, dimetric, and trimetric

Isometric Lines

Isometric lines are lines that are parallel to any of the three isometric axes. These are the most fundamental elements in isometric drawing since they form the skeleton of every isometric shape.

Direction
Parallel to one of the three isometric axes (X, Y, or Z)
Foreshortening
All isometric lines are equally foreshortened to ~82% of true length
Angles on Drawing
They appear at 30 degrees or 90 degrees (vertical) to the horizontal baseline
Measurement
True measurements can only be made along isometric lines using isometric scale

Isometric Planes

Isometric planes are planes that contain any two isometric axes. Any plane parallel to an isometric plane is also an isometric plane. There are three principal isometric planes.

  • Isometric Horizontal Plane: Contains the X and Y axes. Appears as a rhombus with angles of 60 and 120 degrees
  • Isometric Left Plane: Contains the Y and Z axes. Appears as a rhombus inclined to the left
  • Isometric Right Plane: Contains the X and Z axes. Appears as a rhombus inclined to the right
Circles lying in isometric planes appear as ellipses in isometric projection. The standard isometric ellipse has a major to minor axis ratio of approximately 1.73 : 1.

Non-Isometric Lines and Planes

Non-isometric lines are lines that are not parallel to any of the three isometric axes. They do not appear in their true length and cannot be measured directly with the isometric scale.

Non-Isometric Lines
Lines inclined to all three isometric axes. Must be located by finding the endpoints using isometric coordinates.
Non-Isometric Planes
Planes not parallel to any isometric plane. Appear as general quadrilaterals, not rhombuses.
Key Technique
Box method: enclose the object in an isometric box, then locate non-isometric features by coordinates.

Isometric Scale

In true isometric projection, all lengths along the isometric axes are foreshortened to approximately 81.65% of their true length. The isometric scale is used to convert true lengths to isometric lengths.

Isometric Scale Factor = cos(35.27) = 0.8165
A 100 mm true length measures only ~81.65 mm on the isometric scale.
  • Isometric Projection: Uses isometric scale (foreshortened) — theoretically accurate but less common in practice
  • Isometric View/Drawing: Uses true scale (no foreshortening) — slightly enlarged but easier to construct
  • The isometric scale is constructed by drawing lines from true-scale divisions at 45 degrees to the isometric axis direction

Isometric Projection vs Isometric View

These two terms are often used interchangeably but have a precise technical distinction in engineering drawing.

Aspect Isometric Projection Isometric View
Scale Used Isometric scale (0.8165) True scale (1:1)
Size Smaller than actual Larger (~122% of projection)
Accuracy Theoretically correct Practically preferred
Foreshortening Applied to all axes Not applied
Usage Academic / theoretical Industry practice
Also Called Isometric drawing (strict) Isometric drawing (common)

Construction from Orthographic Views

The most practical skill in isometric drawing is converting given orthographic projections (front, top, and side views) into an isometric view.

  1. Study the orthographic views — identify the overall dimensions (L, W, H) and visualize the 3D shape
  2. Draw the isometric axes — establish the vertical axis and two 30-degree axes from a chosen origin point
  3. Construct the isometric box — using the overall length, width, and height, mark distances along the isometric axes to form the enclosing box
  4. Add details incrementally — from the front view, mark features on the right plane; from the top view, mark features on the horizontal plane
  5. Handle non-isometric lines — locate their endpoints using isometric coordinates, then connect them. Never measure directly along non-isometric lines
  6. Remove hidden lines — delete construction and hidden edges. Darken visible outlines to complete the drawing
